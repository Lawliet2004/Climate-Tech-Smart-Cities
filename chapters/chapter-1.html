Chapter 1

The Deep History of Climate Science

Reading time: calculating... | Required Physics Foundations

1. Physics Over Politics

Before discussing artificial intelligence, smart grids, or digital twins, we must establish a foundational truth: the physics of climate change is not a modern discovery, nor is it a political invention. The core mechanics of the greenhouse effect were hypothesized, tested, and quantified in the 19th century by the same physicists who laid the groundwork for thermodynamics and modern computer science.

For computer science students reading this, treat the climate system as a massive, continuous computational loop governed by differential equations. The atmosphere is holding a state variable—atmospheric carbon concentration—that inherently controls the radiative transfer of heat.

2. The Greenhouse Pioneers: Fourier to Tyndall

The story begins in 1824 with Joseph Fourier. Fourier—famous in computer science for the Fourier Transform (the mathematical basis for signal processing, image compression like JPEG, and quantum mechanics)—was the first to calculate that the Earth, given its distance from the sun, should be roughly 33°C (60°F) colder than it actually is. He hypothesized that the atmosphere acts like a transparent insulating box window, letting visible light in but trapping infrared heat.

In 1859, John Tyndall created a highly precise spectrophotometer to test which gases trapped this heat. He discovered that the most abundant gases (Nitrogen and Oxygen, making up 99% of the atmosphere) are completely transparent to infrared heat. The entire "greenhouse effect" keeping the globe warm was being driven by trace gases—primarily water vapor and Carbon Dioxide (CO2).

The Physics: Radiative Forcing
Shortwave radiation (visible light/UV) from the sun passes through CO2 unimpeded. The Earth absorbs this energy and re-emits it as longwave radiation (infrared). The molecular structure of CO2 and Methane (CH4) happens to vibrate at the exact frequency of infrared light, absorbing it and scattering the heat back in all directions—including back down to Earth. This is known as Radiative Forcing.

3. Spotlight: The Forgotten Pioneer

Eunice Newton Foote (1856)

Three years before Tyndall's famous experiments, an American scientist and women's rights campaigner named Eunice Newton Foote conducted a brilliant, elegant experiment. She placed glass cylinders filled with various gases in the sunlight and measured their temperature changes.

She found that the cylinder containing "carbonic acid gas" (CO2) trapped far more heat than standard air, and retained it much longer. She presciently concluded in her brief 1856 paper: "An atmosphere of that gas would give to our earth a high temperature."

Because she was a woman in the 1850s, she was not allowed to read her own paper at the American Association for the Advancement of Science; it was read by a male colleague. Her foundational contribution to atmospheric physics was largely lost to history until it was rediscovered in 2011.

4. Svante Arrhenius: The First Quantification (1896)

In 1896, Swedish physical chemist Svante Arrhenius (who would later win a Nobel Prize) decided to mathematically quantify what would happen if humanity doubled the amount of CO2 in the atmosphere through the burning of coal.

Arrhenius performed thousands of grueling manual calculations representing the radiative transfer of heat across different latitudes. His conclusion? Doubling atmospheric CO2 would raise the global average temperature by 5 to 6 degrees Celsius. Modern supercomputer models operated by AI at organizations like DeepMind arrive at numbers ranging from 2.5°C to 4.5°C. Arrhenius was remarkably close using nothing but paper, pencil, and 19th-century physics.

5. The Keeling Curve & Modern Observation

For over half a century, the theories of Foote and Arrhenius remained mostly academic curiosities. But in 1958, a geochemist named Charles David Keeling began taking hyper-precise, daily measurements of atmospheric CO2 from the Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawaii.

The resulting graph, known as the Keeling Curve, is arguably the most famous chart in all of science. It shows two things:

  1. A jagged, sawtooth pattern every year: The Northern Hemisphere holds most of Earth's landmass and vegetation. When it is Spring/Summer in the North, plants inhale CO2 (lowering global levels). In Autumn/Winter, leaves die and decompose, exhaling CO2. The Earth is literally "breathing."
  2. A relentless upward trajectory: Despite the breathing, the baseline moves relentlessly upward, representing the billions of tons of ancient compressed carbon (fossil fuels) being rapidly injected back into the active carbon cycle by humanity.

    3. 6. Milankovitch Cycles: Why This Isn't "Natural"

    A common misconception is that "the climate has always changed naturally." This is absolutely true. During the Pleistocene epoch, the Earth cycled through dramatic Ice Ages and warm periods. However, we know exactly why this happened, and it has nothing to do with what is happening today.

    The natural glacial cycles are driven by Milankovitch Cycles—slight variations in the Earth's orbit around the sun.

    • Eccentricity: The shape of Earth's orbit stretches from circular to slightly elliptical on a 100,000-year cycle.
    • Obliquity (Tilt): Earth's axial tilt shifts between 22.1° and 24.5° on a 41,000-year cycle.
    • Precession: The Earth "wobbles" on its axis like a spinning top on a 26,000-year cycle.

    These cycles dictate precisely how much sunlight hits the Northern Hemisphere during summer, triggering or ending ice ages over tens of thousands of years. Current Milankovitch geometry suggests the Earth should actually be slowly cooling over the next few millennia.

    Instead, temperatures are spiking violently upward at a rate thousands of times faster than natural orbital forcing. We have overridden orbital mechanics through chemistry.

    7. Knowledge Check

    1. Which property of CO2 and trace gases causes the greenhouse effect?

    Correct! This is called radiative forcing, established by John Tyndall.

    2. What creates the "sawtooth" up-and-down pattern in the Keeling Curve line?

    Correct! The Earth "breathes" as Northern plants absorb CO2 in summer and release it when decomposing in winter.

    3. Which historical figure experimentally proved that CO2 traps more heat than regular air in 1856?

    Correct! Eunice Newton Foote conducted the experiments using glass cylinders in 1856, predating Tyndall.

Key Takeaways

  • The greenhouse effect is 19th-century physics, established by Fourier, Foote, Tyndall, and Arrhenius.
  • Trace gases like CO2 absorb infrared radiation, causing Earth to warm.
  • The Keeling Curve definitively tracks the unnatural rise in global CO2.
  • Milankovitch Cycles drive natural ice ages, but current warming is thousands of times too fast to be orbital.
