Intelligent Transportation Systems

Reading time: calculating... | Urban Mobility Networks

1. Mobility as a Service (MaaS)

Historically, the gold standard of transportation was private car ownership. A car sits parked and useless 95% of its life, yet requires massive amounts of urban space for parking lots and garages.

Mobility as a Service (MaaS) is a shift away from ownership toward access. It integrates public transit, ride-sharing, bikeshare, and scooter rentals into a single, unified digital platform (usually an app like Citymapper or specific municipal apps like Jelbi in Berlin). A citizen can plan a trip that involves taking a train for 5 miles, booking an e-scooter for the "last mile," and paying for it all via a single QR code.

2. Computer Vision for Traffic Flow

Traffic lights are fundamentally "dumb." They generally operate on timed rotations or simple induction loops buried in the pavement that trigger only when heavy metal rolls over them.

Modern Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) deploy edge-computing cameras running computer vision models directly at the intersection. Google's "Project Green Light" analyzes vast amounts of Google Maps driving data to suggest small tweaks to traffic light timings. By smoothing the flow of traffic and preventing cars from idling and rapidly accelerating (the most carbon-intensive phase of driving), early pilots reduced intersection emissions by up to 10%.

More advanced systems prioritize vulnerable road users—keeping the light green a few seconds longer if the camera detects an elderly pedestrian crossing slowly or a cyclist approaching.

3. The Micromobility Missing Link

The majority of car trips in the United States and Europe are under 3 miles, an absurdly inefficient use of a 4,000-pound metal machine to move a 180-pound human. Enter Micromobility (e-bikes, e-scooters, cargo bikes).

Micromobility is fundamentally an IoT software problem. Companies use geospatial ML algorithms to predict where demand will be highest (e.g., placing scooters near a train station precisely when the 5 PM commuter train arrives) and to determine exactly when batteries need swapping by "juicers."

The Danger of Autonomous Vehicles (AVs)
Autonomous vehicles (like Waymo) represent incredible AI accomplishments. However, from a climate and urbanist perspective, they are a massive risk if deployed poorly. If AVs make the act of driving effortless—allowing you to sleep or work during your commute—people will willingly endure three-hour commutes. This will trigger massive urban sprawl and "zero-occupancy" zombie cars circling the block to avoid parking fees. AV tech is best suited for Autonomous Bus Rapid Transit and fixed-route micro-shuttles, not just individual robo-taxis.

4. The Geometry of Public Transit

Technologists often try to invent complex, hyper-expensive solutions (like Elon Musk's Vegas Loop tunnels or Hyperloop) to bypass traffic. However, traffic is a geometry problem, not a technology problem.

  • A car lane moves ~2,000 people per hour.
  • A dedicated bus lane moves ~8,000 people per hour.
  • A protected bike lane moves ~14,000 people per hour.
  • A heavy rail commuter train moves ~50,000 people per hour.

Tech should be used to optimize high-capacity transit (e.g., using AI to dynamically route 15-passenger vans in rural areas without fixed bus stops) rather than trying to optimize low-capacity personal vehicles.

5. Knowledge Check

1. What does Mobility as a Service (MaaS) attempt to achieve?

Correct! MaaS provides access-over-ownership, linking disparate transit networks into one app.

2. Why might cheap, widespread Autonomous Vehicles (robotaxis) actually be bad for the climate?

Correct! The Jevons Paradox strikes again. By lowering the "cost" of the commute (time/attention), total vehicle miles traveled will explode.

Key Takeaways

  • Mobility as a Service shifts urban design away from parking/ownership and toward unified transit access.
  • AI trims intersection emissions by smoothing traffic flow via edge-based computer vision.
  • Micromobility targets the drastically inefficient "under 3 miles" trips historically done in 4000lb cars.
  • Technology cannot beat the sheer geometric efficiency of heavy rail and dedicated bus lanes.
